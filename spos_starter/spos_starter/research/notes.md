## Phase 0 Limiter Analysis — Max Velocity Drop-Off (First-Year Sprinter)

**Question answered:** What are the most likely limiting mechanisms for an athlete with strong acceleration but forced, non-elastic top speed and a 30–60m drop-off?

---

### Mechanism 1 — Leg Stiffness at Max Velocity Contact (PRIMARY)
**Conclusion:** The "forced" top speed description is a stiffness failure. At max velocity, GCT must drop to ~85–95ms. Athletes without adequate ankle-knee complex stiffness absorb energy on contact rather than transmitting it — producing active pawing instead of a reflexive bounce.
- **Evidence level:** High — Morin et al., Hobara et al., Brughelli & Cronin. Leg stiffness strongly correlated with max velocity; low stiffness = longer GCT = lower top speed.
- **What it changes:** Sprint methods must prioritize short, stiff-contact exposures (flying sprints, pogos, ankle-stiff hops) rather than more drive-phase work.
- **Uncertainty:** Exact deficit not measurable without force plate — infer from contact appearance and rep quality.

### Mechanism 2 — Touchdown Timing / Foot Placement vs. COM (PRIMARY)
**Conclusion:** First-year sprinters habitually overstride or touchdown too late in the stride cycle, generating braking impulse. "Reaching" for the ground is a coordination deficit, not a strength deficit.
- **Evidence level:** High — Weyand et al. 2000 (fastest runners apply more force in less time); Mann biomechanics (optimal touchdown ~0.3–0.5 BL in front of CM); Hunter et al. (braking impulse is primary max velocity differentiator).
- **What it changes:** Sprint work must include constraints for earlier, higher-foot touchdown — wicket runs and A-skip/A-march as prep.
- **Uncertainty:** Without video, cannot confirm foot vs. hip flexor timing as root cause.

### Mechanism 3 — Achilles Tendon Stiffness / Elastic Energy Return (SECONDARY, TRAINABLE)
**Conclusion:** At max velocity, ~50% of mechanical energy is stored and returned by the Achilles tendon and plantar fascia. First-year athletes have underdeveloped tendon stiffness. This is the structural basis of mechanism #1 — slow to change, but trainable.
- **Evidence level:** Moderate-High — Kram & Taylor 1990, Roberts et al. (tendon spring mechanics are velocity-dependent); Lichtwark & Wilson (Achilles stiffness is trainable with appropriate loading).
- **What it changes:** Accessory lift selection should use fast, stiff-contact Achilles loading (pogos, drop landings) — not heavy slow calf raises.
- **Uncertainty:** Structural tendon adaptation takes 8–16 weeks. Neural stiffness improvement faster. Don't expect rapid structural change in Phase 0.

### Mechanism 4 — Upright Posture / Trunk Stability Under Speed Stress (SECONDARY)
**Conclusion:** Speed stress in first-year sprinters commonly triggers anterior pelvic tilt, forward lean, or neck/shoulder tension. This disrupts the stiff-spring leg action required at max velocity.
- **Evidence level:** Moderate — Mero & Komi, Nagahara et al. (trunk kinematics influence sprint performance).
- **What it changes:** Sprint sessions require tall posture cues and rep cutoff when posture collapses — posture failure is a stop criterion, not just a cue.
- **Uncertainty:** Difficult to separate from stiffness deficit in practice — both cause visible breakdown at max velocity.

### Mechanism 5 — Hip Flexor RFD / Swing Phase Recovery Speed (TERTIARY)
**Conclusion:** Slow swing recovery constrains stride frequency. However, for this athlete, slow recovery is most likely downstream of long GCT — the leg cannot cycle fast if it is on the ground too long. Not a primary Phase 0 target.
- **Evidence level:** Moderate — Dorn, Schache & Pandy 2012 (hip flexors primary contributors to stride frequency at max velocity).
- **What it changes:** Low Phase 0 priority. Address only if GCT improves but stride frequency remains capped.
- **Uncertainty:** Cannot separate cause from consequence without contact time data.

---

**Overall confidence:** High that stiffness + touchdown timing are co-primary limiters for this athlete profile. The "forced, not elastic" cue is mechanistically unambiguous.
**What still needs testing:** Session 1 will confirm mechanism ranking — if any rep feels elastic, mechanism #1 is the limiter. If all reps feel "reaching," mechanism #2 is primary. Trust feel + video over assumption.
